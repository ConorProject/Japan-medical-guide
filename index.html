📝 Customs Declaration Form - What to Declare?

🎯 Quick Answer: Do I Need to Declare My Medication?

YES, ALWAYS DECLARE if:
  • It's a psychotropic drug (Ritalin, Concerta, Xanax, etc.)
  • It's a narcotic painkiller (codeine, morphine, oxycodone)
  • You're unsure about its classification
  • You have more than personal use amounts
NO DECLARATION NEEDED for:
  • Common OTC medications (Tylenol, Advil, antihistamines)
  • Most antibiotics
  • Birth control pills
  • Vitamins and supplements

Understanding the Difference: Import Certificate vs. Customs Declaration

📋 Import Certificate

Required BEFORE travel for:

  • More than 1 month supply of psychotropics
  • Any amount of certain narcotics
  • Injectable medications

Apply 4-6 weeks before travel

✍️ Customs Declaration

Required AT ARRIVAL for:

  • ANY controlled substance
  • Even if under 1 month supply
  • Even if you don't need a permit

Fill out on the plane/at airport

💊 Example: Methylphenidate (Ritalin/Concerta) - 12 pills for 12 days

📋
Import Certificate: NOT REQUIRED (under 30 days supply)
✍️
Customs Declaration: YES, REQUIRED (it's a psychotropic drug)
📄
What to bring: Original prescription + doctor's letter (just in case)

📝 How to Fill Out the Customs Declaration

1
On the "Prohibited Articles" section:

Check YES for "narcotics, psychotropics, firearms, swords"

2
On the reverse side:

Write: "[Medication name] - [amount] - personal medical use with prescription"

Example:
"Methylphenidate - 12 tablets - personal ADHD treatment with prescription"
3
At customs:
• Go to the RED channel (goods to declare)
• Show your prescription and medication
• Most officers will wave you through quickly
4
Keep ready:
• Medication in original bottle
• Prescription with your name
• Doctor's letter (if you have one)

✅ Pro Tip: Japanese customs officers appreciate honesty. Declaring shows you're following the rules. NOT declaring a controlled substance is risky - it could be seen as smuggling attempt.

📋 Common Medications - Declaration Required?

✅ MUST Declare:
  • Ritalin/Concerta (methylphenidate)
  • Adderall (if somehow bringing with permit)
  • Xanax (alprazolam)
  • Valium (diazepam)
  • Ativan (lorazepam)
  • Codeine combinations
  • Tramadol
  • Any sleeping pills
❌ No Declaration Needed:
  • Ibuprofen (Advil)
  • Acetaminophen (Tylenol)
  • Aspirin
  • Antihistamines (Claritin, Zyrtec)
  • Birth control
  • Antibiotics (most)
  • Blood pressure meds
  • Diabetes meds (non-injectable)

When in doubt, DECLARE IT. It's better to declare unnecessarily than risk being accused of smuggling.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions - Real Answers to Real Concerns

Q: I'm bringing 2 weeks of Ritalin. No import permit needed, but do I still declare it at customs?

A: YES, absolutely declare it. Import permits and customs declarations are completely different things. Ritalin (methylphenidate) is a controlled psychotropic drug in Japan. Even if you're bringing just one pill, you must check "YES" on the customs form where it asks about narcotics/psychotropics. Not declaring could be considered smuggling attempt. The declaration process is quick - most officers just glance at your prescription and wave you through.

Q: Do I need to email first or can I go straight to the online application?

A: You can go straight to online application, but email is safer. The email inquiry is optional but recommended because:

  • Some medications have special requirements not obvious in the forms
  • You'll get confirmation you're using the right process
  • They might tell you about recent changes not yet on the website

However, if you're short on time (less than 3 weeks to travel), skip the email and apply directly online. Most standard applications for common medications (Vyvanse, Concerta, Xanax) are straightforward.

Q: Is a prescription enough or do I need a doctor's letter too?

A: For import permits, you need BOTH. For customs only, prescription might suffice.

For Import Permit:
  • Original prescription ✓
  • Doctor's letter explaining diagnosis ✓
  • Both are mandatory
For Customs Declaration Only:
  • Prescription usually enough
  • Doctor's letter = extra insurance
  • Bring both to be safe

Q: Can I bring some medication with me and mail the rest?

A: NO - This is a common mistake that causes major problems! Japan treats mailed medications very differently from carried medications. Mailing requires a completely different import process through a Japanese importer. Personal carry-on is much simpler. Either bring all your medication with you or arrange prescription filling in Japan. Never try to mail medications to yourself.

Q: My Concerta is generic methylphenidate. Does this matter?

A: No, generic vs brand doesn't matter for import rules. Japanese authorities care about the active ingredient (methylphenidate), not the brand name. Whether it's Concerta, Ritalin, or generic methylphenidate, the rules are identical. Just make sure your prescription clearly shows the active ingredient name.

Q: I take Vyvanse AND Xanax. One application or two?

A: Usually ONE application listing all medications. The online system allows you to add multiple medications to a single application. However, if one is a narcotic (like codeine) and another is a psychotropic (like Xanax), you might need separate applications. The email inquiry (Step 3) is particularly helpful for clarifying this.

Q: What if my application is denied?

A: Denials are rare but here's what to do:

  • Most common reason: Missing or incorrect documents - usually fixable by resubmitting
  • Quantity issues: Reduce amount to 30 days or less
  • Prohibited substance: No fix - find alternatives with Japanese doctor
  • Time running out: Contact your embassy for emergency assistance

Q: What if I lose my medication or need more while in Japan?

A: This is complicated and depends on the medication:

  • Controlled substances: Cannot be prescribed by Japanese doctors to tourists
  • Regular medications: Can see a doctor, but expensive without insurance
  • Best option: Have someone bring it (they need import permit) or fly home early
  • Prevention: Split meds between carry-on and checked bags

Q: I'm just transiting through Japan for 6 hours. Do these rules apply?

A: If you don't clear customs, rules don't apply. If you enter Japan, they do. International transit without entering Japan = no medication restrictions. But if you leave the international transit area (enter Japan even briefly), all medication rules apply in full. Some airports require clearing customs for certain connections - check carefully.

Q: Do I need another permit to take leftover medication OUT of Japan?

A: YES for narcotics, NO for most others. If you're leaving with narcotic painkillers (even your own leftovers), you need an export permit. For psychotropics like Ritalin or Xanax, no export permit needed. Apply for both import AND export permits simultaneously if you're bringing narcotics. This surprises many travelers!
